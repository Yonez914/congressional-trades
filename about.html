A free tool that surfaces every congressional stock trade filed under the STOCK Act — searchable, filterable, and linked back to the original public disclosure. Updated daily.

Why Does This Matter?

Members of Congress sit on committees that write laws affecting the very companies they trade. The STOCK Act requires them to disclose trades — but allows up to 45 days of delay. By the time a filing is public, markets have already moved.

This tool makes those filings searchable and connects each trade to the member's committee assignments. You can judge relevance yourself.

How Do I Use It?

  • Search by member name, stock ticker, or state abbreviation.
  • Filter by chamber (House / Senate) or trade type (purchases / sales).
  • Sort by Disclosure Delay to surface trades that took the longest to report — legally filed but suspiciously late.
  • Expand any card to see the member's committee assignments and a direct link to the original government filing.

Where Does the Data Come From?

Source What it covers
Financial Modeling Prep Daily STOCK Act filings — House + Senate, rolling updates. Includes PTR links to original disclosures.
Senate Stock Watcher Senate trade history back to ~2019, sourced from official Senate eFD filings and aggregated as open data.
unitedstates/congress-legislators Committee assignments for current members of Congress. Public domain, maintained by a community of civic coders.

All trades ultimately originate from official government systems: the House Financial Disclosures portal and the Senate Electronic Financial Disclosure system.

Coverage Notes

These aren't hidden limitations — they're what the underlying public data allows:

  • Senate history — deep coverage back to ~2019 via Senate Stock Watcher, plus ongoing daily updates from FMP.
  • House history — recent trades only. No historical House archive is publicly available in machine-readable form; FMP captures the rolling daily feed.
  • Committee data — current members only. Retired or defeated members will show no committee information, since they no longer hold assignments.
  • Disclosure delay — STOCK Act allows up to 45 days from trade to disclosure. This tool shows the actual delay per trade, so you can see who files promptly and who waits.

A Note on Use

This tool is for transparency research — understanding patterns, conflicts of interest, and committee exposure. It is not a copy-trading tool. By the time a disclosure is public, the trade is 45 days old. Past trades don't predict future ones.
